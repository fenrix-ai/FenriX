Overview

What is Bakery Bash?

Each player runs a grab-and-go café in a plaza food court. Customers walk up, order, and leave — no dine-in. Every round, players set prices, manage employees, bid on ad space, and expand their product menu. A regression model runs under the hood and computes revenue based on each player's decisions. AI-controlled cafés also compete.

Plaza Setting

Core Concept

Professor provides a café dataset. Features like product mix, staff count, pricing, ad spend, and equipment tier map to revenue through a regression model. Players who study the data discover which coefficients matter most.

Dataset-Driven Regression Model Shared Customers AI Competitors

🎯 Revenue is the Goal

Primary metric: cumulative net revenue across all rounds. Secondary metrics include customer count, employee satisfaction, and product diversity score. Two leaderboards — one for the professor (aggregate class stats, model insights) and one for students (competitive rankings, revenue breakdown).

Core Mechanics

Three Pillars of Decision-Making

Every round, players make decisions across three core areas. These inputs feed the regression model.

💰

Pricing

Set prices for each product

Each of the 12 base bakery products has a cost. Players set their own sell price. Price elasticity is baked into the model — too high and customers leave, too low and you bleed margin. The sweet spot changes based on competition and ad presence.

📢

Auctions

Chef, Ads, Technology — 30s each

Three sealed-bid auctions run before each day opens: bid on premium chefs, plaza ad placements, and equipment upgrades. 30 seconds per auction, all blind. Budget spent in one reduces what's available for the next — forcing tradeoffs.

👥

Employees

Hire, manage, poach

Hire bakers, cashiers, and managers. Each has a satisfaction score. Unhappy employees trigger a job-open notification to rival cafés. Competitors can bid to poach your staff. Satisfaction is affected by pay, workload, and events.

Menu

12 Café Products — 3 Categories

Counter-service, grab-and-go. Every café must stock at least one product from each category (1 sweet, 1 savory, 1 drink). Four options per category. Players can buy additional products from their supplier each round.

⚠️

Base requirement: Every café must carry a minimum of 1 sweet pastry, 1 savory pastry, and 1 drink at all times. You can stock up to all 12 products if your budget and equipment support it.

📦 Supplier — New Product Drops

Each round, a supplier offers a new product that players can choose to buy and add to their menu. It costs money to implement, and once added, the next round's dataset shows how it performed — units sold, revenue, customer satisfaction delta. This is how players expand beyond the base 12.

📊 Post-Round Product Data

After each day/round, players receive a time-series dataset showing: how many of each product were purchased, revenue per product, and customer satisfaction score — all indexed by day. This accumulates over rounds so players can spot trends and optimize.

Employees

Hire, Manage & Poach

Every employee has a satisfaction score. This score drives retention, performance, and a competitive poaching mechanic between players.

👨‍🍳

Baker

Produces baked goods. More bakers = more throughput. Tiered: line baker → lead baker → head baker. Higher tiers produce faster with better quality scores.

💳

Cashier

Handles checkout. Speed determines queue length. Long queues = customers walk away. Upgradeable register tech multiplies cashier efficiency.

📋

Manager

Boosts overall satisfaction + handles customer complaints. Reduces employee churn. One manager covers up to 5 staff. Critical for large operations.

😤 Unhappy Employees → Open Job Listing

When an employee's satisfaction drops below a threshold, a message is broadcast to all other cafés that this employee is open to offers. Rival players can bid to poach them. If no one bids, the employee may quit outright. Factors affecting satisfaction: pay rate, workload (too many customers per staff), consecutive rounds without a raise, and AI curveballs.

🤝 Buyout / Poach Mechanic

Players can offer to buy out cooks and employees from rival cafés. This triggers a bidding phase — the original owner can counter-offer with a raise or let them go. Poaching a high-tier baker from a competitor weakens them and strengthens you. Strategic sabotage meets HR management.

Pre-Open Phase

Three Auctions — 30 Seconds Each

Before stores open each round, all players participate in three back-to-back sealed-bid auctions. Each auction lasts 30 seconds. All bids are blind — highest bidder wins. Overbidding in one auction eats into budget for the others.

👨‍🍳

Chef Auction

30 sec · Auction #1

Premium chefs with higher tier skills become available. Bid to hire them before competitors do. Winning a head chef means faster production and higher quality scores.

👨‍🍳 Head Baker — Tier 3 1 avail
👨‍🍳 Lead Baker — Tier 2 2 avail
💳 Senior Cashier — Tier 2 2 avail

Line-level staff (Tier 1) are always available to hire directly — no auction needed.

📢

Ad Auction

30 sec · Auction #2

Limited advertising placements in the plaza. Winners get foot traffic boosts for that round. Presets available for quick bidding.

📍 Plaza Directory 1 slot
🪧 Food Court Sign 2 slots
📱 Social Media 3 slots
🎟️ Coupons 4 slots
⚙️

Technology Auction

30 sec · Auction #3

Premium equipment upgrades with limited supply. Higher-tier tech boosts throughput and reduces breakdown risk. Only top bidders get upgrades this round.

🔥 Industrial Oven 1 avail
🖥️ Self-Checkout Register 2 avail
❄️ Walk-in Fridge 2 avail
☕ Auto Espresso 3 avail
⏱️

Total auction phase: 90 seconds. All three run sequentially before stores open. Budget spent in Auction #1 reduces what's available for #2 and #3 — players must decide where to go aggressive and where to hold back. Tier 1 staff and basic equipment are always available outside auctions.

Gameplay

How a Round Plays Out

Each round follows four phases. After each round, a new row is added to your time-series dataset and the leaderboard updates.

1

Closing Hours

~3 min

Adjust everything: hire/fire staff, change prices, expand menu (buy from supplier), respond to poach offers. Review employee satisfaction. Check public chat for competitor achievements.

2

Triple Auction

90 sec (3×30s)

Three sealed-bid auctions back-to-back: Chef → Ads → Technology. 30 seconds each. Budget spent in one reduces availability for the next.

3

Open for Business

~3 min

Café opens. Customers flow in. Watch real-time metrics: revenue, customers, employee mood. AI curveballs may fire. No changes allowed.

4

Leaderboard + Data

~2 min

Leaderboard updates (revenue + customer satisfaction). New row added to your dataset: revenue, per-product sales, employee satisfaction for that day.

📊 Your Time-Series Dataset Grows Each Round

After each day, your dataset gets a new row with: day number, total revenue, how many of each product were purchased, and employee satisfaction score. This accumulates — by Day 10, you have 10 rows to analyze for trends. On your dashboard you always see three key metrics: revenue, customers, and employee satisfaction.

Competition

Customer Pool & AI Rivals

🧍 Limited Customer Pool

The plaza has a finite number of customers each round. Customers are distributed across cafés based on an attractiveness score computed from:

→ Product variety — more menu items = more customer types attracted
→ Pricing — competitive prices pull more budget-conscious shoppers
→ Ad presence — auction wins directly increase foot traffic share
→ Employee quality — higher-tier staff = faster service = less queue churn
→ Reputation — built over rounds, affected by satisfaction and events

Zero-sum at the margin: If everyone in the plaza invests in ads, the marginal return per ad dollar drops. This teaches competitive strategy — reading the room matters as much as optimizing your own inputs.

🤖 AI-Controlled Bakeries

AI competitors run bakeries alongside human players. They make decisions based on heuristics, creating a baseline of competition even in smaller class sizes.

🟢 Passive AI

Runs a basic café. Stable prices, no ads. Exists as a floor — if you can't beat this, you need to rethink.

🟡 Adaptive AI

Reacts to market conditions. Adjusts prices based on competitor averages. Bids moderately on ads.

🔴 Aggressive AI

Price wars, heavy ad bids, poaches employees. Designed to disrupt. The "boss fight" of the plaza.

Curveballs

AI-Driven Events

Random events that force players to adapt. Each curveball is linked to a player's investment decisions — underspending in one area increases vulnerability.

🔍

Health Inspector

Low cleanliness score → violation → capacity reduced 50% for a round. Invest in cleanliness or pay the price.

📦

Supply Shortage

Key ingredient unavailable (flour, butter, coffee). Over-reliance on one product category = menu items unavailable.

🔥

Viral Trend

A product category surges (matcha goes viral). Players selling it get a traffic boost. Players without it miss out entirely.

⚙️

Equipment Breakdown

Oven or register fails. Cheap equipment = higher failure rate. Premium gear is more reliable. Repair costs money.

🏪

New Plaza Tenant

AI competitor opens nearby — siphons foot traffic. Strong ads + reputation retain more customers.

🎉

Plaza Event

Holiday sale or concert brings extra customers. Players with capacity (staff, inventory) capitalize. Under-staffed bakeries can't serve the surge.

Strategy

Decision Tree — Every Choice & Follow-Up

Each player decision branches into follow-up requirements. This tree maps every choice and what it triggers.

Every decision branches. Setting prices requires checking margins and competitor data. Editing the menu requires meeting the base requirement (1 sweet, 1 savory, 1 drink). Managing employees triggers satisfaction checks and potential poach alerts. Auction bids cascade — overspending on chefs leaves less for ads and tech. After the round, results feed back into the next cycle.

For the Teams

Technical Overview

What each team needs to know to build Bakery Bash.

Front End

Player Dashboard — single-page app with three panels (menu/employees, results/notifications, ads/budget). All decisions submitted via one button.

Real-time Updates — WebSocket feed for live round metrics (customers served, revenue tick, queue length). Leaderboard animates on update.

Plaza Map View — interactive map showing all cafés in the plaza. Hover to see name, revenue, and customer count. Click to inspect competitor details.

Public Chat + Achievements — live chat feed with auto-posted achievements. Filterable by team/café. Players can chat and track competitor milestones.

Notifications — toast system for poach alerts, curveball events, employee warnings. Color-coded by urgency.

Two Leaderboard Views — student-facing (competitive) and professor-facing (analytics). Route-guarded by role.

Back End

Regression Engine — scikit-learn model server-side. Player decisions → feature vector → revenue prediction. Coefficients loaded from professor's dataset.

Customer Allocation — finite pool split by attractiveness score. Compute each café's share per round. Handle ties and edge cases.

Triple Auction System — three sequential sealed-bid auctions (chef, ads, tech) with 30s windows each. Handle simultaneous bids, tie-breaking, budget carry-over between auctions. Broadcast results.

Employee Satisfaction — compute per-employee scores each round. Trigger poach notifications at threshold. Handle bidding/counter-offer flow.

AI Competitors — 3 difficulty tiers with decision heuristics. Run each round alongside human players. Participate in auctions and poaching.

Curveball Engine — probability-weighted random events. Linked to player state (cheap equipment → breakdown). Fire during active rounds.

Time-Series Dataset — after each day, generate a new row per player: day, revenue, per-product unit sales, employee satisfaction. Append to player's cumulative dataset. Serve via API for in-game analysis.

QA

30–50 Player Load — stress test WebSocket connections with full player count. Verify simultaneous bids across all three 30-second auction windows don't race-condition.

Budget Integrity — verify players can't overspend. Test edge cases: exactly $0 remaining, negative after auction, hiring while bidding.

Poach Flow — end-to-end testing of unhappy employee → notification → offer → counter-offer → transfer. Test simultaneous offers from multiple players.

Leaderboard Accuracy — verify revenue tallying across rounds. Test that student and professor views show correct data. Edge: all players tied.

AI Behavior — verify AI competitors make valid decisions. Test that aggressive AI actually disrupts. Confirm AI participates correctly in auctions.

Anti-AFK — test that idle players bleed cash (rent + wages). Verify AFK detection triggers correctly.

Outcomes

What Students Learn

📈 Predictive Modeling

Which features drive revenue? After playing, model coefficients become intuitive. Students experience feature importance through trial and error — then validate with the actual betas.

💡 Resource Optimization

Fixed budget forces concentration on high-impact decisions. Can't invest in everything. Players learn to identify which inputs have the highest marginal return per dollar.

🎯 Competitive Strategy

Shared customer pool teaches market positioning. If everyone bids on Plaza Directory ads, the marginal value drops. Reading competitor behavior is as important as optimizing your own café.

⚖️ Risk Management

Diversified menus, adequate staffing, and premium equipment hedge against curveballs. Students who over-optimize for one metric get punished by variance. The dataset reveals how to build resilience.

🧠 Data-Driven Decision Making

The professor's post-game debrief reveals model coefficients, feature importance rankings, and the gap between student intuition and actual model weights. This is the aha moment.

🤝 Human Capital Management

Employee satisfaction as a game mechanic teaches that talent retention has measurable ROI. Losing a head baker to a competitor has real consequences — students feel the cost of neglecting their team.
